Beyond Engineering

Life outside
the equations.

Engineering is how I think. But there is a lot more that shaped who I am - sport, travel, people, and a few jobs that had nothing to do with aerodynamics.

Sport - where it started

Under 17

I grew up competing. Swimming and skiing agonistically from a young age gave me something I did not fully appreciate at the time - the ability to work hard at something repetitive and unglamorous without losing interest in it. It is a quality that turns out to be useful in engineering too.

But the sport that really formed me was water polo, which I started towards the end of middle school and played for years. I was good enough to be moved between categories constantly - from Under 13 to the first team in Serie B, training and competing alongside players much older than me. I played at national level at Under 15, and one season captained the Under 17 side. What I remember most is not the results but the team itself: a group of people I am still close to today, years after the last match. That kind of belonging - being part of something where you genuinely depend on each other - is something I have tried to find in other areas of my life ever since. When I work in a group, I naturally end up coordinating - not because I push for it, but because I care about the work moving forward.

In my fourth year of high school I quit. The schedules had structured every weekend of my life for as long as I could remember, and I needed to understand what it was like without them. I stopped, picked up kickboxing for a while, and started learning the piano. I gave myself one concrete target: play Yann Tiersen's Comptine d'un autre été at the Christmas concert, six months away. I got there. It is still how I approach most things - not with a vague intention to improve at something, but with a specific goal and a deadline.

The sea and the sky

In the cockpit - flying with my father

I grew up around small airfields - watching him run pre-flight checks, sitting in the cockpit beside him, eventually going up myself. There is a particular feeling to being in a small plane: the noise, the vibration, the way the ground falls away. Long before I understood what aerodynamics was, I was already in love with it. Me and my father also had a real engine failure once, an unplanned landing in a field that my father handled with complete calm, working through each step methodically while I helped him. I did not decide to study aerospace that day. But the memory stayed.

Engine failure - field landing

Alongside flying, the sea has always been part of my life. I used to go sailing from when I was young - proper trips, not day outings - and those experiences gave me a comfort with the open water that has never gone away. I got my Advanced scuba diving certification early and did many dives with tanks. Underwater, there is a quality of attention that the environment demands - total focus, no distractions - that I find hard to replicate anywhere else. From a young age I was also genuinely passionate about fishing, another activity that teaches patience and observation in a very direct way.

Building the drone

First prototype - living room, 2020

The last months of high school happened at home, during the COVID lockdown. I filled the time by building a drone from scratch - not from a kit, but from individual components: a carbon fibre frame, brushless motors, ESCs, a flight controller, a LiPo battery. I walked into a local hobby shop with a rough idea and walked out with second-hand parts the staff sold me at a discount, along with patient answers to every question I had. It took weeks of failed attempts and burnt electronics before it lifted off for the first time. Wobbly, barely controllable - but flying.

I brought the project to my high school final exam, presenting the physics of brushless motors and electromagnetic principles, and flew the drone outside the school building on the day of the exam itself. It was the first time engineering felt like something I had actually made, rather than something I had studied.

Seville and moving through the world

In the second year of my Master's I spent six months in Seville on Erasmus. Academically I followed courses in Heat Transfer and Aircraft Trajectory Optimization. But what I remember most is arriving in a city I did not know, in a language I could manage but not fully own, and finding - surprisingly quickly - that I felt at ease. I went to the Feria de Abril, the most extraordinary festival I have ever seen, the whole city transformed for a week into something you could not quite believe was real. I watched sunsets over the Guadalquivir that I still think about. I made friends from across Europe - people I have since visited in other countries, who have visited me.

When I came back, Seville had changed something in me - not dramatically, but in the way that matters. I was more comfortable with uncertainty, more willing to show up somewhere new without knowing anyone, more curious about the people I had not yet met. I have visited 22 countries in total. Not to collect them, but because moving around, adjusting, figuring out unfamiliar places with unfamiliar people - it suits me.

On choosing how to study

One thing I noticed about myself during the Master's is that I learn differently when there is something to build or test alongside the theory. I deliberately chose courses that included a project component - not because they were easier, but because they were more interesting. Wind tunnel labs, CFD simulations, a 3D-printed bus model, a full flight test campaign with Pipistrel. The theory made more sense when I could see what it was for. I think that is also why the jobs I have done outside engineering have never felt like a distraction - they were another form of learning by doing, just in a completely different context.

Jobs that had nothing to do with aerodynamics

At 17 I worked as a lifeguard at a campsite in Jesolo - my first real experience of responsibility away from home, managing safety for a pool full of strangers every day of the week. I liked it. The directness of it, the fact that you either did the job or you did not, with no ambiguity. During university I worked as a bartender at San Siro stadium on match nights - serving tens of thousands of people in a few frantic hours, moving fast, staying focused in conditions that are genuinely chaotic. I also worked for several months as a waiter at Gattopardo, a Milan club, where the pace was relentless and the margin for error was essentially zero.

None of these have anything to do with aerodynamics. But they taught me things that years of coursework could not: how to read a room quickly, how to stay calm when everything is moving at once, how to be reliable when someone is depending on you and has no patience for excuses. I took these jobs because I wanted them - because I was curious about what that kind of work felt like - and I have never regretted it. Coming back to engineering after a summer of lifeguarding, or a Saturday night at San Siro, always made me appreciate the complexity of what I was studying a little more.

Where things stand

I am finishing my Master's thesis on Flettner rotors and will be done in October 2026. I am looking for roles in aerodynamics, CFD, wind energy or R&D - in Italy or elsewhere in Europe. If you have made it this far and something here resonates, I would be glad to hear from you.
